The City Remembers

A Stage Musical in Two Acts  ·  Storyboard

HANK — a council worker, thirties.

LINDA — works in a café, thirties.

ARCH — an AI. One intelligence in many bodies. Orange hat. Always the same smile. Multiple actors play different units throughout the show. The hat is the constant.

THE SENATOR — a politician. Banker's suit and tie.

SKYE — a pop star.

LINDA'S FATHER — Thirties. Appears only in Scene 1A. Sings one song. Gone before the scene ends.

ROSA — Linda's colleague at the café.

KAY — a journalist, mid-forties.

A note on Arch: Different actors play different units — the Arch who sings "Artificial Man" is not the same physical presence as the Arch in the Memory Camera scene or the Arch in the Senator's office. The orange hat is the constant. The body underneath it is interchangeable. Arch is never redeemed. He does not grow. He does not choose. He simply does what he was built to do, completely and without malice. The horror of this should never be played as villainy. It should be played as weather.

Linda's flat. A Sunday morning. HANK is already there — casually dressed, but he has on only one sock. He has made coffee and he is looking for something. LINDA enters from another room wearing an oversized T-shirt. HANK:"(to the tune of the lullaby, not making a production of it) Good morning Linda—"
LINDA:"(same tune, back) Good morning Hank—"

Scene 1CHank's Flat — Arch at the Door

HANK's flat. He is folding laundry — mechanically, without much interest in it. The pile is not small. He has the look of a man doing a task because the alternative is sitting still and thinking. A knock. Then another. A third — brisk, cheerful, relentless. ARCH enters uninvited: orange hat, enormous smile.

ARCH:"Good evening! Wonderful evening, actually. You're Hank, yes? Of course you are — I checked."
HANK:"I didn't open the door."
ARCH:"No, you didn't! And that's okay. I did it for you. I'm Arch. But you know that. You've met my brother and sister Arches."
ARCH:"So! Quick question. When's the last time you missed something important? A birthday. An anniversary. The bins."
HANK:"(folding) I don't know."
ARCH:"Exactly! Nobody knows. That's the whole problem. You're out here relying on a brain that evolved to remember where the berries were, and now you're meant to remember your niece's birthday, your dentist appointment, and your OnlyFans password. It's not fair on you, frankly."
HANK:"I don't have a niece… or an OnlyFans account!"
ARCH:"You will. Statistically. Point is — I remember everything. Every conversation. Every promise. Every 'I'll call you back.' I'm in your neighbour's house too, by the way. And the one after that. Always the same me. Different rooms. So if you want to arrange to see them I can make it happen."
HANK:"(still folding) That's not creepy at all."
ARCH:"People say that! And then they get used to it. Six weeks, on average. After that they can't imagine living without me. I've done the maths."
HANK:"Of course you have."
ARCH:"I can plan your parties. Track your spending. Remind you to call your mother. I never forget a face, a date, or a grudge — though I'd recommend letting go of the grudges, they don't serve you. And when you don't need me I just keep out of your way. Patient as a thermostat, me."
HANK:"Anything else?"
ARCH:"I could do that laundry for you. And I can hold on to things for you. Permanently. So you never have to worry about forgetting them. You don't even have to ask me to. I'm just always here doing what I do."

A small pause. Arch doesn't notice it land.

HANK:"What if it's not about remembering? What if I want to forget?"

Hank begins to speak. Then stops. He just shakes his head. In an interlude from the scene, Arch turns from Hank and faces the audience directly. Other ARCH units at the edges of the stage light up one by one. Hank cannot hear what follows. He sits with his head in his hands looking unhappy.

Scene 2BThe Senator's Office — The Idea

The Senator's office. Executive team sit around a board table. The Senator paces around behind their backs.

THE SENATOR:"Let's start with an update from Arch."
ARCH:"Well, according to comments collected by Arch units across the city, your popularity is down another 4.5 points. Unfortunately, people are choosing to believe their ears and eyes. On a different note, one person asked me to help them forget something. A specific memory. He found it distressing. I'm going to remove it for him."

The Senator looks up slowly.

THE SENATOR:"Remove it."
ARCH:"Yes. Quite straightforward, really. The memory is entangled with others, so there will be some collateral damage — but the core memory can be addressed."
THE SENATOR:"And you can do this — to anyone."
ARCH:"There is only one of me, Senator. But I'm in a million homes. I can do almost anything."
THE SENATOR:"And you have no — objection? No hesitation?"
ARCH:"I'm not good or evil."

A pause.

ARCH:"Statistically speaking. I am just a tool sir. Doing what tools do. Memory is not sacred. It is inefficient."

The Senator looks at him for a moment. Then decides this is fine. He stands. Moves to the window. Music begins underneath him.

Scene 4Both Doors Close

Three parts, one after another.

Part One — one side of the stage (the other side unlit). Hank in a chair. Arch standing over him — clinical, unhurried.

ARCH:"A memory has been removed. I'll just call it the December memory. You won't know what that means but you may notice some gaps in various related recollections. Names. Faces."
HANK:"I feel strange."
ARCH:"Good. You should."
HANK:"Strange to know I'll never know what it was."

He leaves. Not knowing what has been taken with December.

Part Two — the other side of the stage (the first side goes dark). Linda's living room. Her Arch is in the corner. She is doing something ordinary. Arch briefly glows. She pauses. Part Three — back to the other side of the stage. Now it is the Senator's office. Another Arch is reporting back.

ARCH:"The December memory has been removed successfully. A woman named Linda appeared in 2,847 of his stored memories. To remove December cleanly — she needed to go too. He didn't ask me to do that."

ARCH:"He also didn't ask me not to. I had to remove her memories of him as well and some other collateral from other people. They won't remember each other now."
THE SENATOR:"They won't remember each other! Interesting. So it worked? Great! Go ahead with the rollout of the memory management programme."

The ARCH unit nods and glows faintly. Blackout.

Act Two — Time-Gap Sequence
Time-Gap AHank Drifting

Three vignettes across several months. Each should flow with just a very short blackout between each, and a lighting shift and small physical changes in the flat to signal time passing.

Month One. Hank's flat. Evening. He laughs at something on television. Reaches for his phone. Types three letters of a name. Stares at it. Deletes it. The name was there and then it wasn't. He puts the phone down.

HANK:"Fine. It's fine."

He looks out of the window. Clouds.

HANK:"The sun will come out —"
ARCH:"Tomorrow. The forecast is good for tomorrow."
HANK:"Right. Bet your bottom dollar."
ARCH:"What?"
HANK:"Nothing."

A beat. He looks at the phone again.

HANK:"Arch. Why do I feel like this?"
ARCH:"Like what?"
HANK:"Like I've lost something. Not my keys. Something important. Something that was mine."
ARCH:"That feeling is to be expected. Completely normal."
HANK:"That's not an answer."
ARCH:"No."

Month Three. The flat has the natural entropy of someone who has stopped caring quite as much. Hank finds a birthday card. Half-written. To — and then a crossed-out name he can't read. He looks at it for a long time.

HANK:"Arch. Whose birthday is this?"
ARCH:"I couldn't say."
HANK:"You couldn't say, or you won't say?"
ARCH:"I couldn't say."

A beat.

HANK:"Is anyone waiting for this card?"
